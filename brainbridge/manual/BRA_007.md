It’s a **semantic bridge** between raw neural activity and structured knowledge graphs, purpose-built for senior architects, indie hackers, and deep-tech researchers who need to externalize complex reasoning with surgical precision. ## Detailed Content

### What is brainbridge?

brainbridge is a **real-time, bidirectional thought-connection engine** that ingests fragmented ideas (voice, text, sketches) and automatically constructs a navigable, version-controlled mind map. Unlike note-taking apps that flatten context, brainbridge preserves the *spatial and temporal relationships* between concepts, enabling you to traverse your own cognitive landscape like a debugger steps through code.

### Core Features (Hardcore)

1. **Semantic Graph Auto-Linking with Latent Drift Detection**  
   Every node you create is vectorized on-device using a lightweight transformer. brainbridge then cross-references your entire graph to surface *latent connections* you haven’t consciously made. If your thinking drifts over time (e.g., from “microservices” to “actor model”), it flags the divergence and suggests a refactor of your mental model.

2. **Temporal Replay & Branching**  
   Your thought stream is recorded as a directed acyclic graph (DAG). You can **rewind** to any point in time, fork a branch, and explore an alternate line of reasoning—then merge it back. This is not a timeline; it’s a full version control system for your brain, complete with diffs and conflict resolution for contradicting ideas.

3. **Context-Aware Export to Executable Pipelines**  
   brainbridge doesn’t just spit out Markdown. It can export a selected subgraph as a **YAML workflow** (for CI/CD), a **TypeScript state machine**, or a **Pydantic schema**. Your fragment of thought becomes a deployable artifact, ready to plug into your existing SaaS architecture.

### How to Use (Step-by-Step)

1. **Initialize a Brainbridge Session with a Seed Node**  
   Run `brainbridge init --session "microservice-migration"`. This creates a local graph database (SQLite + vector index). Drop your first thought: `"Eventual consistency vs. strong consistency in payment flows"`. The system immediately vectorizes it and assigns a temporal ID. Use `brainbridge status` to verify the node was indexed and check its embedding hash.

2. **Fork a Reasoning Branch for A/B Testing**  
   Discovered a contradiction? Run `brainbridge fork --parent <node_id> --branch "optimistic-vs-pessimistic"`. This creates a child node that inherits all parent context. Add your counterpoint: `"Pessimistic locks kill throughput in high-read workloads"`. Now you have two parallel reasoning lines. Use `brainbridge diff` to see the semantic delta between them.

3. **Merge Branches and Export as a Decision Record**  
   Once you’ve resolved the conflict (e.g., decide on “optimistic with retry”), run `brainbridge merge --into "microservice-migration" --resolution "choose-optimistic"`. The system creates a merge node with a changelog. Finally, export: `brainbridge export --format yaml --output decision-record.yaml`. This YAML can be fed into your infrastructure-as-code pipeline as a design rationale.

### Best Practices

1. **Use Temporal Replay for Post-Mortems**  
   After a production incident, replay the decision branch that led to the problematic code. brainbridge’s **temporal replay** lets you walk through the exact sequence of thoughts (including discarded alternatives) that preceded the bug. This transforms a blame game into a systemic learning tool.

2. **Auto-Export Every Merge as an ADR (Architecture Decision Record)**  
   Configure brainbridge to run a post-merge hook: `brainbridge hook --on-merge "export --format adr --to docs/adr/"`. This ensures every major reasoning branch is automatically documented as a formal ADR, linking back to the original thought graph. Your team can trace any architectural decision back to the exact moment of reasoning.
