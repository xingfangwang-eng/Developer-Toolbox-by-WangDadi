# cineskin - CIN_008

## Overview

cineskin is a next-gen film-grade skin retouching & stylization engine, purpose-built for video editors and colorists who demand frame-consistent, non-destructive workflows. Unlike generic beauty filters, cineskin leverages a hybrid AI pipeline—combining semantic segmentation, temporal coherence tracking, and LUT-based color mapping—to apply "skin theory" from cinematography directly onto moving footage. It’s not a filter; it’s a digital dailies colorist for skin.

## Core Features

### 1. Temporal Skin Lock (TSL)
Unlike single-frame retouching that flickers across cuts, TSL uses optical flow analysis to track skin regions across a timeline. It maintains consistent luminance, saturation, and micro-texture across all frames, eliminating the "pulsing" artifact common in AI beauty tools. You can set a reference frame, and cineskin propagates the exact skin grade across the entire clip—even through occlusions and motion blur.

### 2. Cinematic Skin LUT Generator
This feature extracts the skin tone curve from any reference film still (e.g., a frame from *Blade Runner 2049* or *The Grand Budapest Hotel*) and generates a custom 33x33x33 3D LUT. It then applies that LUT exclusively to skin regions (via the segmentation mask), leaving background and wardrobe untouched. The result: a character’s skin matches the emotional tone of the scene without affecting the overall grade.

### 3. Subsurface Scattering (SSS) Emulation for Video
cineskin simulates how light penetrates and scatters beneath the skin—a hallmark of high-end VFX. Using a multi-scale Gaussian blur stack and a custom diffusion kernel, it adds realistic warmth to edges (ears, nose, fingers) and reduces the "plastic" look from over-sharpening. You can dial in scatter radius and intensity per shot, mimicking the behavior of real film stock under tungsten or LED lighting.

## How to Use

### Step 1: Import & Mask Calibration
Drag your clip onto the timeline. cineskin auto-detects faces and body skin in the first frame. Open the **Mask Inspector** and verify the segmentation overlay. If the AI misses a hand or an ear, use the **Brush + Keyframe** tool to paint the missing area, then set a keyframe. The temporal tracker will propagate this correction across the clip. For best results, ensure your footage is at least 1080p and well-lit (no crushed blacks on skin).

### Step 2: Apply a Cinematic Skin LUT
In the **Grade Panel**, click **Load Reference** and select a film still (PNG/DPX/EXR). cineskin analyzes the skin pixels in the reference and generates a LUT. Click **Apply to Clip**. Now open the **LUT Mix** slider: set it to 0.8 for a subtle influence, or 1.0 for a full transfer. Use the **Skin Range** curve to limit the LUT to specific luminance zones (e.g., only midtones) to avoid blowing out highlights.

### Step 3: Fine-Tune Subsurface Scattering
Go to the **SSS Panel**. Set **Scatter Radius** to 4–6 pixels for close-ups (head-and-shoulders), or 2–3 pixels for wide shots. Adjust **Warmth** to 0.3 (tungsten) or 0.6 (daylight). Check the **Temporal Smooth** checkbox to prevent flickering. Play the clip at 50% speed and inspect edges—if you see a "glow halo," reduce the **Blur Passes** from 3 to 2. Export as ProRes 4444 or DNxHR 444 to preserve the SSS data.

## Best Practices

### 1. Always Grade Skin Before Background
In a typical color pipeline, you grade the whole image first, then isolate skin. With cineskin, reverse the order: apply your cinematic skin LUT and SSS adjustments **before** the primary color grade. This ensures skin retains its natural texture and doesn't get crushed by aggressive contrast or saturation layers later. Use cineskin’s **Pre-Grade Mode** to lock skin adjustments as a pre-node in your Resolve or Premiere timeline.

### 2. Use Temporal Keyframes for Scene Transitions
When cutting between two vastly different lighting setups (e.g., indoor tungsten to outdoor overcast), don’t rely on automatic tracking. Manually set a **Scene Change Marker** at the cut, then keyframe the Skin LUT and SSS parameters on each side of the marker. This prevents the AI from trying to "blend" the skin grade across the transition, which would result in a muddy, washed-out look. Export the clip as a flattened reference and re-import if needed.
