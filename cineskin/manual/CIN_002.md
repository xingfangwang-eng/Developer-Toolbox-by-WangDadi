# cineskin - CIN_002

## Overview

cineskin is a next‑generation **AI‑driven film skin & texture editor** built for post‑production pros who need pixel‑perfect control over cinematic grading, without the overhead of a full NLE. It bridges the gap between real‑time preview and offline‑grade quality, targeting motion designers, colorists, and VFX artists.  
**Keywords**: *Movie skin design, Video effects tool, Movie post‑production*

## What is cineskin?

cineskin is a standalone, GPU‑accelerated tool that applies **per‑shot, adaptive skin‑tone grading** and **texture‑preserving film looks** directly onto video frames. Unlike traditional LUT‑based workflows, cineskin uses a lightweight neural network to isolate skin regions, maintain natural micro‑detail, and composite film grain that respects luminance and chroma noise. It’s built for pipelines where speed and consistency across hundreds of shots are non‑negotiable.

---

## Core Features (Hardcore)

1. **Adaptive Skin‑Isolation Engine**  
   Leverages a custom‑trained CNN (trained on 50k+ film frames) to segment skin with sub‑pixel accuracy. It automatically adjusts for mixed lighting, partial occlusion, and varying skin tones—no manual rotoscoping or garbage masks needed. Outputs a separate alpha channel for downstream compositing.

2. **Per‑Channel Film Grain Synthesis**  
   Generates grain that matches the chosen film stock’s spectral response. You can independently control grain size, contrast, and color bleed per RGB channel. The algorithm uses a **3D noise‑shaping filter** to avoid posterization and banding in shadows and highlights.

3. **Real‑Time Look‑Dev with LUT‑Free Transform**  
   Instead of baking a LUT, cineskin applies a **matrix‑based color transform** that preserves skin‑tone vectors. You can tweak hue, saturation, and luminance of skin vs. background independently—all at 4K 60fps on a mid‑range RTX card. No pre‑rendering, no proxies.

## How to Use (Step‑by‑Step)

### Step 1: Load & Pre‑Analyze Your Shot  
Import your video file (ProRes, DNxHR, or EXR sequence). Click **Analyze** to let cineskin run a one‑pass inference on the entire clip. This generates a per‑frame skin mask and extracts luminance histograms. Wait for the “Mask Ready” indicator—typically 2–3 seconds per 4K frame on an RTX 4070.

### Step 2: Tune Skin & Background Separately  
Open the **Dual‑Layer Color Panel**. For the skin layer, adjust *Hue Offset* (e.g., +3° toward amber) and *Saturation Gain* (0.8–1.2). For the background, use *Luma Curve* to crush shadows or lift mids. Enable **Split‑View** (left: original, right: processed) to compare in real time. Fine‑tune the mask edge with the *Feather* slider (recommended: 0.5–1.5 pixels for 4K).

### Step 3: Apply & Export Final Grain  
Switch to the **Grain Lab** tab. Select a film stock preset (e.g., Kodak 2383, Fuji 3510). Adjust *Grain Intensity* (0–100) and *Chroma Bleed* (0–100). Click **Render Final** to export a 16‑bit TIFF or DPX sequence with embedded grain. For batch workflows, use the **Command‑Line Batch Renderer** included in the install.

## Best Practices (Industry)

1. **Always calibrate your monitor to Rec. 709 or DCI‑P3 before grading** – cineskin’s color transforms assume a known display profile. If your monitor drifts, the skin‑isolation engine may mis‑segment due to color temperature shifts. Run the built‑in *Display Calibration Check* once per session.

2. **Use the “Grain First” workflow for VFX plates** – When compositing CGI onto live‑action, apply cineskin’s grain **before** your final composite. This ensures the grain pattern matches the plate’s noise floor and prevents double‑grain artifacts. Export a grain‑only pass as an alpha‑keyed EXR for your compositor.
