# cineskin - CIN_023

## Overview

cineskin is a next-gen video effects engine built for post-production workflows that demand precision, speed, and cinematic skin-grade aesthetics. It bridges the gap between real-time color grading and deep-learning-driven texture enhancement, enabling editors to apply consistent, filmic skin tones across entire sequences without manual LUT stacking. Keywords: *Movie skin design, Video effects tool, Movie post-production*.

## Why cineskin?

Traditional skin retouching tools either flatten dynamic range or introduce temporal artifacts. cineskin solves this by leveraging a lightweight neural inference pipeline that operates directly on YUV vectors, preserving highlight rolloff and shadow detail while applying skin-specific color transforms. Think of it as a colorist’s scalpel, not a sledgehammer.

## Core Features

### 1. Adaptive Skin Masking with Temporal Coherence
cineskin’s mask generator doesn’t rely on chroma key or edge detection alone. It uses a hybrid model combining semantic segmentation (trained on 50K+ film frames) with optical flow tracking. This means skin masks stay stable across cuts, dissolves, and fast motion—no flickering, no bleeding onto wardrobe or backgrounds.

### 2. Non-Destructive Film Emulation Layer
Instead of baking in a look, cineskin applies a separate film emulation transform that operates on the skin region only. You can fine-tune grain density, halation, and color crossover per shot, while the rest of the frame remains untouched. It’s like having a dedicated film print LUT per face.

### 3. Real-Time Waveform Preview for Skin Tones
Stop guessing if your skin tones fall within broadcast-safe or theatrical ranges. cineskin overlays a live waveform monitor that isolates the skin region’s luminance and chrominance vectors. You can spot illegal values, cross‑channel bleed, or color casts before rendering—saving hours of QC later.

## How to Use

### Step 1: Import Sequence and Calibrate Skin Reference
Load your timeline clip into cineskin. Use the **“Pick Reference”** tool to sample a neutral skin area (forehead or cheek). The engine will auto‑detect the dominant skin cluster and generate a three‑dimensional mask. Adjust the **“Edge Feather”** parameter (0–100) to control mask softness—start at 35 for most close‑ups.

### Step 2: Apply Film Emulsion with Log‑Space Mapping
Navigate to the **Film Layer** panel. Select a stock preset (e.g., *Kodak 2383* or *Fuji Eterna*) and set the **“Exposure Offset”** to –0.3 stops to retain highlight detail. Enable **“Grain Lock”** to synchronize noise patterns with the clip’s original grain—this prevents double‑texturing when compositing.

### Step 3: Validate with Live Skin Waveform
Toggle the **Waveform Overlay** (shortcut: `W`). The isolated skin region appears as a vector trace. Ensure the skin tone line falls within the **I‑Q vector box** (green zone). If it drifts into magenta, reduce the **“Crossover Gain”** slider by 0.1 increments until the trace centers. Export with **ProRes 4444** for final delivery.

## Best Practices

### 1. Always Mask First, Grade Second
Many editors apply color grading globally and then attempt skin correction—this results in muddy masks and blown‑out highlights. Instead, run cineskin’s skin detection *before* your primary grade. The neural mask will adapt to exposure changes, allowing you to push contrast in the background without affecting skin integrity.

### 2. Use Grain Lock for Multi‑Camera Edits
When cutting between cameras with different sensor noise profiles (e.g., ARRI Alexa vs. Sony Venice), cineskin’s *Grain Lock* feature matches the film emulation grain to each camera’s native texture. This eliminates the “digital mismatch” look that cheapens high‑budget sequences. Always enable it before rendering the final timeline.
